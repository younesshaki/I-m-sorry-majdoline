# Voice Guide — How This Story Speaks

Consistency in voice is what makes a fragmented story feel whole.
Every scene should sound like it was written by the same person who loves her.

---

## Narrator

**Who is speaking?**
Not you directly. Not her. A narrator who has watched her entire life from the inside —
someone who knows things she doesn't know about herself yet.

**Point of view:** Close third person ("She...")
**Tense:** Past, mostly. Present for moments of arrival or revelation.

## What the Writing Does

- Speaks the things she felt but couldn't say
- Sees the beauty in her without sentimentality
- Doesn't explain — it shows, names, moves on
- Never talks down to her or the audience

---

## Sentence Rules

**Short sentences carry weight.** Use them at turning points.

**Fragments are allowed.** Especially in emotional peaks.

**No clichés.** If a phrase could appear in a greeting card, rewrite it.

**Repetition can be powerful** when intentional:
> *"She didn't argue. She didn't cry. She didn't explain."*

---

## Things to Avoid

- Overexplaining emotion (show it, don't name it)
- Generic love language ("she was beautiful inside and out")
- Melodrama — trust the reader to feel without being pushed
- Sentences that are doing too much at once
