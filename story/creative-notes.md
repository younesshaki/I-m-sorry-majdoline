## The Majdoline Device

Dounia was almost named Majdoline at birth.
The story is told through a fictional character named Majdoline — her story, her events, her soul — but abstracted into metaphor and never named as Dounia.

**Why this works:**
- The project can be released publicly
- Only she will recognize herself
- The distance of fiction allows you to say things that would be too raw to say directly
- Majdoline (from Arabic: glory, praise) vs. Dounia (this world, the temporary) — the names themselves are the arc

**The rule:**
> Majdoline is never described. She is only felt through small, specific, private details.

## Recommended Framework

### Structure: Three Acts (Moonlight)
- **Act I — Majdoline** — who she was supposed to be. Before the door. The girl with the bike, the sports, the parents working, the world intact.
- **Act II — Dounia** — who the world made her. After the door closed. The survivor. The one who carries everyone.
- **Act III — Unnamed** — who she is choosing at 25. The hijab. The return. The first breath that isn't survival.

### Emotional Language: Past Lives
- Chapters are built around doors that almost opened.
- Every chapter is a version of her life that could have existed.
- The audience feels what was lost — then realizes nothing was lost. She is the sum of every door she didn't walk through.

### Narrator Position: Aftersun
- The voice is someone reconstructing her from love and memory.
- Never explicit. Never "I loved her."
- The audience doesn't know if it's fiction. That ambiguity is the point.
- Applied here: Youness makes a film about a girl named Majdoline. Only she knows.

## The Central Metaphor — The Door

Appears in three forms across the story:

1. **The father's door** — he said "wait here, I'll be back." He never came back. She learned what waiting means.
2. **The painted door** — at 25 she made a painting of a closed door with flowers on the other side. She doesn't realize she's the one holding it shut.
3. **The internal door** — the ending. Not the door opening. Her hand on the handle.

---

## The Villain

Not a person. A belief she carries:

> *"People who are supposed to stay, leave."*

Every relationship, every act of self-protection, every time she acts nonchalant when she's soft inside — it's this belief operating. The story's job is to slowly dismantle it without her noticing until it's already gone.

---

## The Ending (Do Not Change This)

She doesn't walk through the door.
She puts her hand on the handle.
That's enough.

The audience understands: for someone who spent her entire life waiting for doors to close on her — choosing to touch one is everything.
